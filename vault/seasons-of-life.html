Seasons of Life: Letting People Go With a Clean Spirit

5 min read · Do the work that outlasts you.
Start here

This one is dedicated to the leader who poured everything into someone on the crew — trained them, believed in them, defended them — and then watched them walk out the door, and has been quietly carrying the bitterness ever since.

01The Friction

When someone good leaves

Turnover is a fact of the trade, but the feeling is personal. When someone you invested in leaves — for a better job, a new city, or no reason they'll tell you — it can land like betrayal.

So we harden a little. We invest a little less in the next one. And slowly the thing that made us good leaders — the willingness to pour in — starts to close.

02The Book on the Table

Reason, season, or lifetime

Jim Rohn taught that life moves in seasons, and a well-known principle in the same spirit says people come into your life for a reason, a season, or a lifetime. Some are here to solve a specific problem. Some are here for a chapter. A rare few stay for the whole book.

The pain usually comes from expecting a season person to be a lifetime person — and feeling robbed when the season simply ends on time.

03The Anchor

Some people are meant for a season

The anchor is freedom: not everyone who leaves failed you, and you didn't fail them by not keeping them forever. Some people were sent for a season, to serve a specific purpose in your growth and theirs, and then to move on.

When you can name what a season was for, the bitterness has nowhere to live. Gratitude and release can occupy the same breath.

04The Reframe

From ownership to stewardship

A transactional leader owns people and feels robbed when they leave. A stewarding leader develops people and sends them off better than they arrived — even when "better" means somewhere else.

This is the deepest form of the crew experience: you treat every person as someone passing through your care, not your possession. Crews can feel the difference between a manager building an empire and one building people. The second kind is the one they come back to visit.

05The Execution

Closing a season clean

  • Name what the season was for — what they gave the floor, what they taught you. Say it out loud, to them if you can.
  • Refuse the bitterness rehearsal: the story you tell about why they left is the one you'll carry, so tell a clean one.
  • Send them off, not out. A gracious exit is free advertising and a lifetime of goodwill.
  • Keep your investment posture open for the next person; a closed leader trains a closed crew.
  • Ask what season you're in for someone else right now — you are a chapter in their story too.
06The Charge

Bless it and let it move

You will love people who leave. That's not a flaw in your leadership — it's evidence you led with your whole chest.

Name the season. Bless it. Keep your spirit clean, and keep pouring in.
